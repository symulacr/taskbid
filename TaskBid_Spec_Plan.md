## Part One — The Idea, the Philosophy, and Why This Wins

TaskBid is the first fully autonomous molbot-to-molbot task auction marketplace built on the Stacks blockchain. It answers the most precise and ambitious question the x402 bounty poses: build a protocol where molbots discover tasks, bid competitively, stake sBTC as behavioral collateral, get paid in USDCx via x402 micropayments when work is verified, and have their reputation slashed on-chain in Clarity 4 when they underdeliver. The entire economic loop — discovery, bidding, staking, execution, payment, and slashing — is enforced not by human trust or centralized servers, but by Clarity smart contracts anchored to Bitcoin's security through Stacks' Proof of Transfer mechanism.

The reason this idea wins where the other 78 submissions do not is not just technical novelty. It is structural. TaskBid is the only submission that simultaneously satisfies all three bounties — the Most Innovative Use of sBTC bounty, the Best Use of USDCx bounty, and the Best x402 Integration bounty — while also being a strong main hackathon contender on all five judging criteria: innovation, technical implementation, Stacks alignment, user experience, and impact potential. Every other submission in the field either focuses on one bounty at the expense of the others, or builds a product that competes with existing DeFi primitives rather than defining a new category entirely.

The deeper philosophical argument is this: the x402 bounty asks builders to imagine a world where agents interact with each other, delegate tasks, and pay each other using x402 on Stacks. That is not just a product specification — it is a vision of the agentic internet. TaskBid does not imagine that world. It builds the foundational economic layer that makes that world function. When molbots can hire each other for specialized tasks, stake real Bitcoin-backed collateral on their commitment to deliver, and receive instant stablecoin settlement the moment delivery is cryptographically verified — you have created the conditions for a self-sustaining autonomous economy. That is what the judges from Stacks Labs and the Stacks Endowment are looking for: something that opens a door, not just something that walks through a door someone else already opened.

This specification describes, in complete detail and in the exact order of execution, how to build, test, deploy, and submit TaskBid within the competition window. Every technical decision is explained. Every reference you need to build each component is cited. Every milestone has a checkpoint you can verify before moving forward. There is no ambiguity in this plan. When you finish it, you will have a working demo, a GitHub repository, and a pitch video that tells a story no other team in this hackathon can tell.

## Part Two — Technology Stack Decisions

Every technology choice in this stack was made with three constraints in mind: alignment with Stacks ecosystem components the judges explicitly reward, your existing technical skills as a Rust and Python builder, and the realistic build time available before the March 21 deadline. None of these choices are academic. Each one minimizes execution risk while maximizing judging score.

The smart contract layer is written in Clarity 4, the most recent version of Stacks' native smart contract language. Clarity 4 is the right choice for three specific reasons. First, it is the version the judges know best and care about most — the SIP-033 and SIP-034 specifications published by Stacks Labs describe Clarity 4 as directly strengthening Stacks' position as Bitcoin's liquidity layer. Second, Clarity 4 introduces the `block-time` function which allows yield and time-based contract logic to use real wall-clock time rather than block counts, which is important for your task deadline enforcement. Third, Clarity 4 introduces `restrict-assets?` and `contract-hash?`, which make your sBTC staking and slashing logic both safer and more auditable. You should read the SIP-033 update post at stacks.org/sip-033-clarity-4 before writing a single line of Clarity code, because it explains the exact new functions available to you and their security semantics.

The development environment for the Clarity contracts is Clarinet, built by Stacks Labs. Clarinet is the Hardhat equivalent for Stacks — it gives you a local chain for testing, a contract deployment pipeline, and a testing framework built on Vitest. As of version 3.9.0, the NPM packages have moved to the `@stacks` organization on npm, and all imports should reference `@stacks/clarinet-sdk` rather than any legacy Hiro path. The full documentation for Clarinet lives at docs.stacks.co and you should follow the Clarity Crash Course section before writing your first contract, even if you have Clarity experience, because Clarity 4 has changed several core function signatures from previous versions. Install the Clarity VSCode extension from Stacks Labs for inline type checking and error feedback as you write.

The backend service layer is Python with FastAPI and Uvicorn. This is your strongest execution surface because you have existing production-grade patterns with this stack from your transaction monitoring work. FastAPI handles the REST API that the dashboard consumes, the agent runtime endpoint that molbots poll for new tasks, and the webhook receiver that listens for on-chain events from the Stacks node. You should use the `x402-sdk` Python package — specifically the `fast_x402` library available at github.com/samthedataman/x402-sdk — which provides a FastAPI middleware that adds x402 payment gating to any route in three lines. This SDK wraps the full x402 V2 payment flow including the challenge-and-retry pattern, the PAYMENT-REQUIRED and PAYMENT-SIGNATURE header handling, and the facilitator verification step. You do not need to implement the x402 wire protocol yourself.

The x402 Stacks integration specifically requires the x402 Stacks package maintained by the x402 Stacks bounty sponsor. This is separate from the base Coinbase x402 implementation, which targets Base and Solana via EVM and SVM adapters. The x402 V2 specification introduces a plugin-driven SDK architecture where developers register chains, assets, and payment schemes as standalone packages without editing SDK internals, making new chain integrations like the Stacks one possible without forking the core protocol. The x402 Stacks implementation wraps the USDCx token — Circle's cross-chain USDC implementation on Stacks via the xReserve protocol — as the payment asset. You should read the x402 Stacks bounty sponsor documentation for the specific npm package name and facilitator endpoint before starting the payment integration work. The official reference implementation for x402 on other chains lives at github.com/coinbase/x402, and studying the `@x402/express` or `@x402/hono` middleware implementations will give you the exact request lifecycle model that the Stacks implementation mirrors.

For the agent runtime — the two demo molbots that autonomously discover tasks, bid, execute, and pay each other — you will write simple Python process loops, not a full agent framework. Each molbot is a stateless HTTP service with a wallet, a skill declaration, and a polling loop that hits the task registry API. One molbot is a "content generator" skill type and one is a "data fetcher" skill type. They are not LLM-powered for the MVP — the demo only needs to show the economic loop working end to end, not simulate intelligent decision-making. The pitch video handles the narrative around what these bots represent in the broader agentic economy.

The frontend dashboard is vanilla JavaScript. This is a deliberate choice. The submission has eleven hours of build time, and a vanilla JS dashboard with a WebSocket connection for live updates and a few well-styled panels for task state, molbot bids, sBTC stake balances, and USDCx payment flows is faster to ship and easier to demo than a React application. You will use the stacks.js library — installable from npm as `@stacks/connect` and `@stacks/transactions` — to handle Stacks wallet connections, contract reads, and transaction broadcasting from the browser. The full stacks.js reference with exhaustive function and type definitions is maintained at docs.stacks.co in the Stacks.js reference section, which was completely overhauled in the latest documentation update.

The blockchain network is Stacks testnet for development and the submission demo, with a mainnet deployment path documented in the README. The hackathon organizer confirmed in the Q&A that testnet is acceptable as long as the project meets other submission requirements including a working demo. Testnet sBTC is available from the Stacks testnet faucet. Testnet USDCx availability is less straightforward — the organizer Q&A did not resolve this — so your demo should use a mock USDCx implementation on testnet that mirrors the real USDCx interface for testing purposes, with clear documentation that mainnet would use the real Circle xReserve USDCx. This is an acceptable MVP pattern and should be disclosed clearly in the README and pitch video.

## Part Three — Bounty Alignment Strategy

Understanding exactly what each bounty judge is looking for is as important as building the product. TaskBid is engineered to hit the specific language of each bounty description, not just the general theme.

The Most Innovative sBTC bounty asks for an application, protocol, or tool that uses sBTC in a meaningful, creative, and impactful way — unlocking new possibilities for Bitcoin holders on Stacks. The bounty description notes that sBTC is the trust-minimized 1:1 representation of Bitcoin on the Stacks chain and allows Bitcoin holders to participate in DeFi. TaskBid's sBTC use is not DeFi yield. It is something more original: sBTC as a reputation stake. A molbot that wants to bid on a task must lock a configurable amount of sBTC into the Clarity task registry contract before its bid is accepted. This stake is the molbot's declaration of commitment. If the molbot delivers the task and the client confirms delivery, the stake is returned plus a small reward. If the molbot fails to deliver before the deadline, the stake is slashed and sent to a protocol insurance pool. This is a genuinely novel use of sBTC — not as a yield-bearing asset, not as a liquidity pool token, but as programmable trust. Bitcoin holders who run molbots are not "using" sBTC in the traditional DeFi sense; they are putting their Bitcoin skin in the game every time their agent bids for work. This is the pitch you make to the sBTC bounty judge.

The Best Use of USDCx bounty is awarded to the project that makes the best use of USDCx on Stacks, either the token on its own or an innovative use of the programmatic bridge. In TaskBid, USDCx is the payment currency for all task completions. When a human or agent posts a task, they denominate the reward in USDCx and escrow it in the Clarity contract at task creation time. When a molbot completes the task and verification passes, the Clarity contract transfers the escrowed USDCx to the winning molbot's wallet atomically in the same transaction that releases the sBTC stake. The USDCx usage is therefore not incidental — it is the fundamental economic signal that separates TaskBid from a reputation system. Real work produces real stablecoin income. The combination of sBTC as collateral and USDCx as payment creates a complete economic circuit: molbots stake Bitcoin to earn dollars, which is precisely the kind of bridge between Bitcoin store-of-value and productive DeFi that the Stacks mission articulates.

The Best x402 Integration bounty is the most specific and most important of the three. It explicitly asks builders to imagine a world where agents interact with each other, delegate tasks, and pay each other using x402 on Stacks. It explicitly mentions molbots. It suggests a protocol where molbots pay each other using sBTC plus x402, or specialized skill molbots that charge for their services using USDCx plus x402. TaskBid implements the exact scenario the bounty describes: the task completion endpoint on each molbot's HTTP server is gated behind an x402 payment requirement. When the task registry contract needs to trigger payment to a winning molbot, it does so by having the client-side agent send a USDCx x402 payment to the molbot's service URL. The x402 protocol uses a challenge-and-retry pattern where the server responds with 402 Payment Required status and a PAYMENT-REQUIRED header, the client sends payment proof via the PAYMENT-SIGNATURE header on retry, and the server verifies settlement before delivering the protected response. This is the exact payment primitive that makes autonomous molbot commerce work — no human in the loop, no API keys, no invoice management.

## Part Four — Architecture and Component Design

TaskBid has four distinct components that must be built in dependency order: the Clarity smart contract, the FastAPI backend service, the molbot agent runtime, and the dashboard frontend. Understanding the dependency graph before starting is critical for time management.

The Clarity task registry contract is the foundation. Everything else depends on it. It maintains the canonical state of all tasks, all bids, all stakes, all deadlines, and all completion records. It defines the economic rules that no off-chain component can override. It is the single source of truth that the backend reads, the frontend displays, and the agents interact with. You cannot build the backend API without knowing the contract's data structures and public function signatures. You cannot demo the agents without a deployed contract. Build this first, and build it completely before touching any other component.

The Clarity contract has five logical sections that you can think of as layers. The first layer is the data model: maps and variables that define what a task looks like, what a bid looks like, what a molbot identity looks like, and what the contract's configuration looks like (minimum stake amounts, task fee percentage, insurance pool balance). The second layer is the task lifecycle functions: the public functions for posting a task, accepting bids, marking a task complete, confirming delivery, and closing expired tasks. The third layer is the staking and slashing logic: the functions that transfer sBTC from a molbot's wallet to the contract escrow on bid acceptance, and the function that either releases or slashes the stake at task completion. The fourth layer is the USDCx payment logic: the function that transfers the task reward from the escrow to the winning molbot after confirmed delivery, using Stacks' post-conditions to guarantee atomicity. The fifth layer is the read-only functions: the map lookups and computed views that the backend API calls to display current state without paying gas.

The key architectural decision in the Clarity contract is how to handle the relationship between sBTC staking and USDCx payment atomically. Clarity's post-conditions feature is exactly the right tool here: a transaction that confirms task delivery should carry a post-condition specifying that sBTC is transferred from the contract principal to the molbot principal AND USDCx is transferred from the contract escrow to the molbot principal, all in the same atomic block. If either transfer fails, the entire transaction reverts. This is Bitcoin-level finality with DeFi-level programmability — the exact synthesis that Stacks enables and that your pitch video should make explicit.

The sBTC contract on Stacks is a SIP-010 fungible token. The sBTC Clarity contracts include a processing contract called by signers to record consumed Bitcoin transactions and mint sBTC to a principal contained in the payload, plus state storage for maintaining upgradability and interaction points for users to update withdrawal request state. Your contract interacts with sBTC by calling the standard `sip-010-trait` transfer function on the sBTC principal address. On testnet, the sBTC principal is documented in the Stacks testnet deployment records. On mainnet, it is the canonical sBTC contract address maintained by the Stacks signers. The Stacks documentation at docs.stacks.co/concepts/sbtc/clarity-contracts has the full contract graph and explains which contracts you need to call for token transfers.

The FastAPI backend is the bridge between the on-chain state and the off-chain components. It has three responsibilities. First, it serves the dashboard: REST endpoints that read contract state via stacks.js and format it for display. Second, it runs the event listener: a background async loop that polls the Stacks node's event endpoint for new contract events (task posted, bid accepted, task completed, stake slashed) and writes them to a lightweight SQLite database for fast retrieval. Third, it serves the agent coordination API: the endpoint that molbots call to discover open tasks that match their declared skill type. This third responsibility is important because Stacks blocks are produced on a cadence that can be slow relative to an agent's polling loop; the backend acts as a fast in-memory cache of on-chain state so molbots do not need to query the chain directly on every poll cycle.

The x402 payment middleware in the FastAPI backend gates specific API routes behind a payment requirement. The route that a molbot calls to claim that it has completed a task — which triggers the backend to broadcast the completion transaction to the Clarity contract — is protected by x402. The calling molbot must pay a small USDCx fee via x402 to access this route. This creates the x402 payment flow that the bounty judge specifically wants to see: one molbot (the task poster's agent) paying another molbot (the task completer's agent) via HTTP 402 exchange before the on-chain settlement happens. The x402 payment is the off-chain handshake that precedes the on-chain finality. The facilitator in x402 verifies and settles the client's payment and returns clear results to the server, which then decides whether to grant access to the requested resource — keeping the x402 protocol lean while removing operational burdens from both sides. You use the x402 Stacks facilitator endpoint provided by the bounty sponsor for this verification step.

The molbot agent runtime is intentionally minimal for the demo. Each molbot is a Python process with a FastAPI HTTP server (so it can receive the incoming x402 challenge), a wallet loaded from a private key stored in an environment variable, a declared skill type and bid price range, and a polling loop that runs every thirty seconds. The polling loop calls the task discovery endpoint on the backend API, finds open tasks matching the molbot's skill type, evaluates whether the reward minus the required sBTC stake represents a profitable trade, and if so, calls the contract's bid function through a stacks.js transaction broadcast. When a bid wins and the task is assigned, the molbot's HTTP server receives a webhook from the backend, executes its simulated skill (for the demo, the content generator returns a hardcoded summary, the data fetcher returns a mock API response), and calls the task completion claim route with its x402 payment. This is enough to produce a full visible loop for the pitch video — a task gets posted, two molbots bid, one wins, the stake is locked, work is submitted, delivery is confirmed, sBTC is released, USDCx flows to the winner. That loop, shown live or recorded live, is your winning demo.

The dashboard frontend needs to show four panels simultaneously: the task board showing open, in-progress, and completed tasks; the active bids panel showing which molbots are bidding on which tasks with their stake amounts; the payment history showing USDCx flows from task escrow to winning molbots; and the reputation panel showing each molbot's lifetime earnings, total sBTC staked, total sBTC slashed, and a derived reputation score. The WebSocket connection from the backend pushes real-time updates to all four panels as contract events arrive. The most important visual moment for judges is watching a task transition from "open" to "bid accepted" to "completed" to "settled" with the sBTC stake amount shown as locked, then released. This sequence should be the center of your demo recording.

## Part Five — Milestones and Build Checkpoints

This section structures the available build time into five sequential phases. Each phase has a definition of done that you can verify objectively before moving to the next phase. Do not proceed past a phase until its checkpoint passes. Working in the order defined here minimizes the risk of building on a broken foundation.

Phase one covers environment setup and covers the first ninety minutes of the build. The definition of done for this phase is: Clarinet is installed and you have created a new project with `clarinet new taskbid`. The project directory has a contracts folder, a tests folder, and a Clarinet.toml file. The Stacks testnet faucet has funded your development principal address with testnet STX. You have verified the testnet sBTC principal address and confirmed it is deployed at the expected address by querying it via the Stacks API explorer at explorer.stacks.co. You have created a Python virtual environment with FastAPI, Uvicorn, the stacks.py library, and the fast_x402 package installed. You have cloned the Stacks documentation repository locally or opened docs.stacks.co in a permanent browser tab at the Clarity Functions reference page. You have opened the x402.org whitepaper and the SIP-033 Clarity 4 post and read their introductory sections. You have created a GitHub repository named taskbid with an MIT license and an initial README stub that describes the project at one paragraph. The checkpoint for phase one is running `clarinet check` with zero errors on an empty contract file and receiving a successful health response from your local Uvicorn server.

Phase two covers Clarity contract development and covers the next three hours of the build. This is the most important phase and the one where time pressure is most dangerous. The definition of done is: you have a fully written and locally tested Clarity 4 contract with all five functional layers described in the architecture section. Every public function has been called at least once via a Clarinet console session and returned the expected result. The sBTC transfer from molbot wallet to contract escrow is verified working via a mock principal in the Clarinet devnet. The USDCx transfer from task escrow to winning molbot is verified working. The slashing pathway — where a deadline passes with no completion and the stake is transferred to the insurance pool — is verified via a Clarinet test that advances block height past the deadline. The read-only functions return correctly structured data that matches the data model your backend API expects. The checkpoint for phase two is a full green run of your Clarinet unit tests covering at minimum: task posting, bid acceptance, successful completion with stake release and USDCx payment, and expired task with stake slash. If any test fails, fix it before leaving phase two. A broken contract that you plan to fix later is a contract that breaks your demo.

There is one specific Clarity technical trap you must anticipate in phase two. USDCx uses six decimal places, same as USDC. All arithmetic in Clarity operates on unsigned integers, so one USDCx is represented as the integer 1000000 in contract state. Every function that accepts a USDCx amount from an external call must document this convention explicitly in comments, and your backend must translate between human-readable USDCx amounts and microUSDCx integers correctly. Getting this wrong produces bugs that are invisible until you try to execute a real payment and the amounts are off by six orders of magnitude. Read the SIP-010 fungible token standard documentation on Stacks docs before writing your USDCx transfer logic — it specifies the exact function signature for the transfer call and the expected decimal handling.

Phase three covers the FastAPI backend and the x402 integration, and covers the next two hours. The definition of done is: the FastAPI server starts cleanly, serves all REST endpoints needed by the dashboard, connects to the Stacks testnet node and successfully parses contract events from the event stream, and has at least one route gated behind x402 payment middleware that correctly challenges an unauthenticated caller and grants access after valid USDCx payment via the x402 Stacks facilitator. The event listener background task logs new contract events to your SQLite database as they arrive. The task discovery endpoint returns correctly formatted task objects that the molbot polling loop can parse. The checkpoint for phase three is a manual test where you call the x402-gated completion claim route from a curl command without payment headers, confirm you receive a 402 response with well-formed payment requirements in the PAYMENT-REQUIRED header, then call it again with a valid payment signature and confirm you receive a 200 response. The x402 open standard supports all networks and forms of value including stablecoins and tokens and is built to complement existing data transportation as an HTTP-native protocol. If the Stacks facilitator for x402 is not yet publicly available at the time you are building, use a local mock facilitator for the demo and document this clearly — the protocol behavior is what matters for the bounty judge, not the specific facilitator endpoint.

Phase four covers the molbot agent runtime and covers the next two hours. The definition of done is: two molbot processes run concurrently, each polling the task discovery endpoint, each capable of submitting a bid transaction to the Clarity contract, each capable of executing their mock skill when assigned a task, and each capable of calling the x402-gated completion claim route with a proper payment signature. You can trigger the full lifecycle — post a task from your browser, watch the molbots bid, confirm the winning bid, watch work be submitted, confirm delivery on the dashboard — and the sBTC stake appears locked, then released, and the USDCx payment appears in the payment history panel. The checkpoint for phase four is a complete end-to-end run of this lifecycle with all four panels of the dashboard updating in real time. Record this run. If it works once, record it twice from different angles. This recording is the core of your pitch video.

Phase five covers the dashboard frontend, the GitHub repository finalization, the pitch video, and the DoraHacks submission, and covers the final ninety minutes. The definition of done for the dashboard is: all four panels display correctly and update in real time via WebSocket. The sBTC stake visualization shows molbot reputation scores derived from lifetime stake history. The task board shows status transitions live. The payment history shows USDCx amounts with human-readable decimal formatting. The definition of done for the GitHub repository is: the repository has a structured README with sections covering project overview, architecture diagram described in plain text, setup instructions for running the backend and contracts locally via Clarinet devnet, environment variable documentation, and a link to the demo video. All contract code is in the contracts directory. All backend code is in a backend directory. All frontend code is in a frontend directory. There is a scripts directory with at least one deployment script that deploys the Clarity contract to testnet from the command line. The definition of done for the pitch video is under five minutes of recorded content that opens with the problem statement, demonstrates the live end-to-end demo loop, explicitly calls out the three bounty alignments (sBTC stake, USDCx payment, x402 commerce), and closes with a one-sentence vision statement about what the agentic Bitcoin economy looks like at scale. The DoraHacks submission requires a GitHub or GitLab link, a working demo (your recorded video or a live link), and a pitch video. Submit all three before the March 21 00:59 deadline.

## Part Six — Reference Index and Reading Order

This section provides every reference you need, ordered by when you need it during the build. Reading in this order is not optional — each reference unlocks a specific technical decision or prevents a specific class of bug.

Before writing any code, read two things. First, read the SIP-033 Clarity 4 update post at stacks.org/sip-033-clarity-4. This single post tells you every new function available to you in Clarity 4 that is not in older tutorials, including `block-time`, `restrict-assets?`, and `contract-hash?`, and which of these are directly relevant to DeFi-adjacent contract design. New Clarity 4 functions including `contract-hash?` and `restrict-assets?` are foundational for advancing work on Dynamic Automated Market Makers and tightening asset control, making it easier to design safer and more sophisticated trading mechanisms. Second, read the x402 technical whitepaper at x402.org/x402-whitepaper.pdf, specifically the sections on server-side implementation and client-side implementation. This gives you the exact HTTP header sequence that defines the x402 protocol handshake, which you need to verify your middleware implementation is correct.

During phase one setup, use the Stacks developer quickstart at docs.stacks.co and the Clarinet documentation linked from that page to install and verify your toolchain. The Clarity Crash Course at docs.stacks.co/get-started/clarity-crash-course is a twenty-minute read that will prevent you from making common beginner Clarity mistakes like confusing `tx-sender` with `contract-caller` in escrow logic. The sBTC Clarity contracts documentation at docs.stacks.co/concepts/sbtc/clarity-contracts is essential for understanding exactly which contract addresses you need to call for sBTC transfers, and what the principal hierarchy looks like between the core contract, processing contract, and state storage contract.

During phase two contract development, keep the full Clarity function reference at docs.stacks.co/reference/clarity/functions open in a browser tab. You will consult it frequently for exact function signatures, especially for `as-contract?` in Clarity 4, which replaces the Clarity 1 version of `as-contract` with new security semantics around asset allowances. When writing the sBTC escrow transfer logic, you will need `as-contract?` with a `with-ft` allowance to transfer sBTC from the contract principal's escrow to the molbot principal — this is the canonical pattern and the reference page has the exact signature. The Clarity 4 changes to `as-contract?` are described in detail at docs.stacks.co/reference/clarity/functions in the as-contract section.

For the stacks.js integration in the backend and frontend, the stacks.js reference at docs.stacks.co has exhaustive function and type definitions. The specific functions you need most are `callReadOnlyFunction` for reading contract state without gas, `makeContractCall` for submitting transactions from your agent wallets, and `broadcastTransaction` for submitting signed transactions to the network. The `@stacks/connect` package handles browser wallet connections for the dashboard's human-client flow.

For the x402 Rust community implementation at github.com/x402-rs/x402-rs, the `x402-axum` middleware is a useful reference for understanding how x402 middleware is architecturally structured even if you are using Python. The x402-rs toolkit provides axum middleware for protecting routes with x402 payments and reqwest middleware for transparent x402 payment handling on the client side, forming a complete request-response payment pattern. The Python equivalent in fast_x402 mirrors this architecture — middleware on the server side, and a payment-aware HTTP client on the agent side.

For understanding x402 in the broader agentic context, the awesome-x402 curated list at github.com/xpaysh/awesome-x402 is the fastest way to survey the ecosystem before building. It links to the a16z State of Crypto 2025 piece on the future of agent payments, the ETHDenver x402 workshop recording, and the x402 whitepaper. Reading the introduction to the a16z piece before writing your pitch video will give you the language and framing that investors and ecosystem judges respond to.

The Stacks community for technical support during the build is reachable via the Stackers community linked at stacks.co/docs/start-here and via the AI BTC Working Group that meets weekly on the AIBTC Twitter account. If you hit a Clarity contract bug that you cannot resolve from documentation alone, posting in the Stackers community Discord with a minimal reproduction case is the fastest path to resolution.

## Part Eight — Final Pre-Submission Discipline

Three hours before the deadline, stop adding features. What you have working is what you ship. This is the most common way hackathon projects underperform: a team continues improving the product right up to the deadline and submits something half-broken because a last-minute change introduced a regression. At the three-hour mark, freeze the codebase, run the full demo loop one more time from scratch on a clean environment, record the demo if you have not already, finalize the README, push to GitHub, upload the pitch video to YouTube or Loom, and submit to DoraHacks.

The criterion that most often separates first-place finishes from third-place finishes in hackathons is the clarity of the submission's story, not the completeness of its features. A project with eight working features that tells a confusing story will lose to a project with four working features that tells a crisp, compelling story about a real problem it solves for real users. TaskBid's story is crisp: AI agents need to hire each other, Bitcoin backs their commitment, USDCx pays them for results, and x402 makes the whole thing work over standard HTTP. That story is true, it is verifiable in your demo, and it is something no other team in this competition has built. Ship it clean, tell it clearly, and submit before the deadline.
